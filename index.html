HISTORY
In the months between 1972 and 1973,
the newborn Nike (Blue Ribbon Sport until 1970)
launched a high-cut leather shoe with a
nylon mesh tongue and a vulcanized rubber sole.
Nothing too special.
A simple shoe on which was applied
(stroke of genius) the Nike Swoosh
in extra large size.
The size of the logo was studied to be large
enough to be remembered, but not big enough
to seem disproportionate.
A bit "like a woman's skirt: long enough not to
be provocative and, at the same time,
short enough to make turn heads"
(the quote comes directly from the '70s,
when people didn't pay too much attention
to politically correct).

The Blazer and the NBA


Another crucial step for the shoe's success
was to contract George "Iceman" Gervin
as a testimonial. Gervin was one of the best
players of the NBA of those years, and the
return of image for Nike was crazy. Accomplice,
and here we step back to the aforementioned
stroke of genius, the mega logo imprinted on the
shoes that appeared in all its "splendor" in every
photo taken at The Iceman during his actions
on the court.Soon, however, the decline came.
Innovation in those years was fast and Nike,
thanks to the work tracked by Bill Bowerman,
represented the epicenter ofthat technological
earthquake that was breaking down American sport
and beyond. A few years after launching the Blazer,
Nike revolutionized the sportsindustry with
the introduction of its famous Air. It is easy
to understand who paid the duty.

Skaters choose the Nike Blazer


Besides, how could a rubber sole compete
with an air unit that seemed to come
from the future?
But it is precisely the difficulties that
distinguish the winners from the losers.
Even at the cost of questioning everything.
And so the Blazer was forced (so to speak)
to leave the basketball courts to find
comfort in the world of skateboarding.
The rubber sole, so "derided" on the parquet,
seemed to be made on purpose to guarantee
stability and grip on the board.
